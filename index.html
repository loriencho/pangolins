The Pangolin

The very trait with the purpose of protecting pangolins has led to the species' endangerment: its tough, protective scales.

About

The pangolin, sometimes called the "scaly anteater," is an animal only the size of an average housecat! According to the International Union for the Conservation of Nature (IUCN), there's almost no information available on population levels of any species of pangolin, but they are all thought to be in decline.

Poaching

The pangolin is mostly trafficked in Asia (mainly in China and Vietnam), but its trafficking is increasing in Africa. Their meat, which is considered a delicacy, and scales, which are used in traditional medicine, leads to its high demand.

Behavior

When pangolins feel threatened, they will curl into a tight ball to protect themselves, much like a roly-poly. If caught, they thrash about to slice the predator with their sharp scales and release a foul-smelling fluid from their glands.

Diet

The pangolin hunts at night as it prefers to stay in its den during the day. Using its keen nose, it sniffs out ant and termite nests and then digs them up withs its sharp claws. The pangolin then eats using its tongue, which can be up to a stunning 41 centimeters long!
